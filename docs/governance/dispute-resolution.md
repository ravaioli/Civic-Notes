# Dispute Resolution

Disagreements are a natural part of any community. How we handle them determines whether conflict weakens our bonds or strengthens them. CivicNotes is designed to surface and resolve disputes constructively, with processes that are transparent, fair, and grounded in restorative principles. This guide explains the full dispute resolution framework—from informal conversations to formal Council review.

---

## Principles of Dispute Resolution

| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Start locally** | Resolve issues at the closest level to those involved before escalating. |
| **Assume good faith** | Most people are trying to help, even when they make mistakes. |
| **Focus on impact, not intent** | Understand how actions affected others, not just what was intended. |
| **Seek understanding first** | Before proposing solutions, ensure everyone feels heard. |
| **Be transparent** | Processes and outcomes should be clear to those involved and, where appropriate, the community. |
| **Restore relationships** | The goal is not punishment but healing and improved future interaction. |
| **Learn and adapt** | Each dispute is an opportunity to improve our processes. |

## Dispute Resolution Ladder

Disputes move through escalating levels of formality. Most are resolved at lower levels.
```markdown
Level 1: Direct Conversation
↓ (if unresolved)
Level 2: Facilitated Discussion
↓ (if unresolved)
Level 3: Guild Circle (if within a guild)
↓ (if unresolved)
Level 4: Mediation (Moderation Council involvement)
↓ (if unresolved)
Level 5: Formal Council Review
↓ (if necessary)
Level 6: Appeal to Guild Council
```

| Level | Description | Who Facilitates |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **1: Direct Conversation** | Parties attempt to resolve one‑on‑one. | The parties themselves |
| **2: Facilitated Discussion** | A trusted third party helps conversation. | Another guild member, friend, or colleague |
| **3: Guild Circle** | Structured group conversation within a guild. | Guild facilitator or elder |
| **4: Mediation** | Formal mediation with Council support. | Moderation Council member |
| **5: Council Review** | Full Moderation Council hears the case. | Moderation Council |
| **6: Guild Council Appeal** | Appeal of Council decision. | Guild Council |

## Level 1: Direct Conversation

### When to Use
- Minor misunderstandings.
- Disagreements about a specific note or action.
- Situations where a private conversation might clear the air.

### How to Do It

1. **Reach out privately.** Use CivicNotes' direct message feature or another agreed channel.
2. **Use "I" statements.** "When you said X, I felt Y" rather than "You always do X."
3. **Listen to understand.** Ask questions about the other person's perspective.
4. **Look for common ground.** What do you both agree on?
5. **Propose a way forward.** This might be agreeing to disagree, or a small change in behavior.

### Example

> "Hi Jamie, I saw your comment on my note about the traffic study. I think we might be talking past each other. Would you be open to a quick chat to clarify?"

### If It Works

- No further action needed.
- Both parties may choose to post a brief note acknowledging resolution (optional).

### If It Doesn't Work

- Either party may suggest moving to Level 2.
- If one party refuses to engage, the other may escalate.

---

## Level 2: Facilitated Discussion

### When to Use
- Direct conversation didn't resolve the issue.
- The parties need help communicating.
- Power imbalance makes direct conversation difficult.

### Choosing a Facilitator

The facilitator should be:
- Trusted by both parties.
- Neutral (not already involved in the dispute).
- Skilled in listening and communication.

Facilitators might be:
- Another guild member (if within a guild).
- A mutual contact.
- A community elder or respected user.

### Process

1. **Agree on a facilitator.** Both parties must consent.
2. **Set a time and format.** Video call is often best; text can work.
3. **Establish ground rules.** No interruptions, respectful tone, focus on issues.
4. **Each party shares their perspective.** Facilitator ensures both are heard.
5. **Identify underlying interests.** What does each person really need?
6. **Brainstorm solutions.** Facilitator helps generate options.
7. **Agree on next steps.** This might be a written agreement.

### If It Works

- Document the agreement (privately, or publicly if both agree).
- Both parties may choose to close the matter.

### If It Doesn't Work

- Facilitator may suggest moving to Level 3 (if within a guild) or Level 4.

---

## Level 3: Guild Circle (For Guild‑Internal Disputes)

### When to Use
- Dispute involves multiple guild members.
- Affects guild functioning or culture.
- Lower levels haven't resolved the issue.

### What Is a Guild Circle?

A circle is a structured conversation format used in many restorative justice traditions. It ensures everyone has a chance to speak and be heard.

### Circle Process

1. **Preparation.** Circle keeper (usually a guild facilitator) talks to each participant beforehand.
2. **Opening.** Circle keeper explains purpose and guidelines.
3. **Check‑in.** Each person shares how they're feeling.
4. **Storytelling.** Each person shares their perspective on the dispute, uninterrupted.
5. **Reflection.** Participants reflect on what they heard.
6. **Identifying needs.** What would help resolve this?
7. **Agreement.** If consensus reached, document it.
8. **Closing.** Check‑out round.

### Circle Guidelines

- Speak from the heart.
- Listen with respect.
- No interruptions.
- What's said in circle stays in circle (unless agreed otherwise).

### If It Works

- Document agreement in guild records.
- Circle keeper may follow up to ensure implementation.

### If It Doesn't Work

- Guild may refer the matter to Moderation Council (Level 4).

## Level 4: Mediation (Moderation Council Involvement)

### When to Use
- Dispute crosses guild boundaries.
- Guild‑level processes have failed.
- Code of Conduct concerns (mediation may still be attempted before formal review).

### Requesting Mediation

Any party may request mediation via the **"Request Moderation"** form, selecting "Mediation Request." The form should include:

- Parties involved.
- Summary of the dispute.
- What lower‑level resolution attempts have been made.
- What outcome is sought.

### Mediation Process

1. **Assignment.** A Moderation Council member is assigned as mediator.
2. **Initial contact.** Mediator contacts all parties to explain process.
3. **Information gathering.** Mediator reviews any relevant evidence (notes, messages).
4. **Joint session (optional).** If appropriate, mediator facilitates a conversation with all parties.
5. **Caucusing (optional).** Mediator may meet with parties separately.
6. **Agreement.** If resolution reached, mediator documents agreement.
7. **Follow‑up.** Mediator checks in after agreed period.

### Mediator's Role

- Neutral facilitator, not judge.
- Helps parties find their own resolution.
- Does not impose solutions.

### If It Works

- Agreement is documented (confidential unless parties agree otherwise).
- Case closed.

### If It Doesn't Work

- Mediator may recommend moving to Level 5 (Council Review).
- Any party may request formal review.

## Level 5: Council Review

### When to Use
- Mediation failed or is inappropriate.
- Serious Code of Conduct violations.
- Disputes requiring interpretation of rules.
- Appeals of lower‑level decisions.

### Filing a Request for Review

Use the **"Request Moderation"** form, selecting "Formal Review." Provide:

- All parties involved.
- Detailed description of the issue.
- Evidence (links, screenshots, messages).
- What lower‑level resolution attempts were made.
- What outcome is sought.

### Initial Review

Within **72 hours**, a Council member determines:

- Is this within Council jurisdiction?
- Have lower‑level remedies been exhausted?
- Is there sufficient information to proceed?

If accepted, the case enters the docket. If rejected, the filer receives explanation and may appeal to full Council.

### Case Assignment

Cases are assigned to a **primary reviewer** (one Council member) who:

- Gathers additional information.
- Contacts all parties.
- Attempts to facilitate resolution (if not already tried).
- Prepares findings for full Council.

### Investigation

Primary reviewer may:

- Request additional evidence.
- Interview parties (individually or together).
- Consult with other Council members.
- Hold a virtual hearing (rare, for complex cases).

Timeline: **14–30 days**.

### Deliberation

Primary reviewer presents findings to full Council. Council discusses and votes.

### Decision

Decision includes:

- **Findings of fact**.
- **Interpretation** of relevant rules.
- **Ruling** (e.g., promise upheld, sanction imposed).
- **Rationale**.

Decisions are published in the **Case Log** (with personal information redacted).

### Possible Rulings

| Type | Examples |
|------|----------|
| **Promise dispute** | Uphold, overrule, remand for re‑verification |
| **Code of Conduct** | Warning, suspension, ban, feature restriction |
| **Guild dispute** | Recommendation, referral to Guild Council |
| **Verification appeal** | Uphold denial, overturn, remand |
| **Admin appeal** | Uphold, overturn, modify |

## Level 6: Appeal to Guild Council

### When to Use
- Party believes Council made procedural error.
- New evidence unavailable during review.
- Council decision misinterpreted rules.
- Sanction is disproportionately severe.

### Appeal Process

1. **File appeal** within **14 days** of Council decision.
2. Appeal must specify grounds (procedural error, new evidence, misinterpretation, disproportionality).
3. Guild Council reviews appeal at next regular meeting.
4. Guild Council may:
   - **Uphold** Council decision.
   - **Remand** to Council for reconsideration with guidance.
   - **Reverse** decision.
5. Guild Council decision is final.

### Grounds for Appeal

| Ground | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Procedural error** | Council failed to follow its own processes, affecting outcome. |
| **New evidence** | Material evidence not previously available that could change outcome. |
| **Misinterpretation** | Council incorrectly interpreted Code of Conduct or other rules. |
| **Disproportionality** | Sanction clearly disproportionate to violation. |

## Special Case: Promise Disputes

Promise disputes follow a slightly specialized path.

### During Verification

If a promise is disputed during the verification period:

1. Dispute flag raised → Verification paused.
2. Moderator reviews within **48 hours**.
3. Moderator may:
   - **Reject dispute** – Verification resumes.
   - **Uphold dispute** – Promise status changes to Disputed; enters Level 5 review.
   - **Request more information** – Verification period extended.

### After Verification

If a verified promise is later challenged:

1. Any user can file a dispute (Level 5 review request).
2. Council reviews evidence and may:
   - **Uphold** – Promise remains verified.
   - **Overrule** – Promise becomes Rejected.
   - **Modify** – Promise description adjusted (rare).

## Special Case: Guild Disputes

### Internal Guild Disputes

1. Guild should attempt Levels 1–3 first.
2. If unresolved, guild may request Council mediation (Level 4).
3. If mediation fails, Council may:
   - Issue non‑binding recommendations.
   - If governance breakdown threatens guild function, recommend revocation to Guild Council.

### Inter‑Guild Disputes

1. Guild leaders should attempt direct conversation (Level 1).
2. If unresolved, they may request Council mediation (Level 4).
3. If mediation fails, Council may issue recommendations.
4. If dispute involves interpretation of platform policies, Council may refer to Guild Council for policy clarification.

## Special Case: Code of Conduct Violations

### Reporting

Anyone may report a Code violation via:

- **"Report" button** on any content (note, comment, profile).
- **"Request Moderation"** form.

Reports should include:
- What happened (with links/evidence).
- When it happened.
- Why it violates the Code.

### Initial Triage

Within **24 hours**, a Council member reviews:

- Is this a potential violation?
- Is there immediate harm requiring urgent action?

If urgent (e.g., harassment, threats), temporary measures (e.g., content removal, account suspension) may be taken pending full review.

### Investigation

Follows Level 5 process, with emphasis on:

- Gathering evidence.
- Hearing from all parties.
- Determining facts.

### Sanctions

See [Moderation Council](moderation-council.md) for sanction guidelines. Sanctions are graduated and proportional.

### Restorative Options

Where appropriate, Council may recommend restorative measures:

- Apology (public or private).
- Educational resources.
- Mediation with affected party.
- Restorative circle.

## Restorative Justice Approach

CivicNotes embraces restorative justice principles. This means:

- **Harm is acknowledged.** What happened and who was affected?
- **Responsibility is taken.** Not blame, but accountability.
- **Needs are addressed.** What do those harmed need? What does the person who caused harm need to take responsibility?
- **Community is involved.** How can the community support healing?
- **Future focus.** How do we prevent recurrence and rebuild trust?

Wherever possible, the resolution process seeks to restore relationships, not just punish violations.

## Transparency and Confidentiality

### What Is Public

- Final Council decisions (redacted).
- Case summaries in annual report.
- Aggregated statistics.

### What Is Confidential

- Individual testimony.
- Deliberations among Council members.
- Identifying information of parties (except in cases where parties are public figures and information is already public).

### Exceptions

Confidentiality may be waived:
- By consent of all parties.
- When necessary to protect the community from ongoing harm.
- When required by law.

## Rights of Parties

Throughout the process, parties have the right to:

- **Be heard.** Present their perspective and evidence.
- **Be informed.** Know the status of their case.
- **Be treated respectfully.** No harassment or retaliation.
- **Have support.** Bring a supporter to meetings (not as advocate).
- **Appeal.** Challenge decisions as provided.
- **Confidentiality.** Within limits above.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need to try lower levels before filing with the Council?
Yes, in most cases. The Council will typically reject cases where lower‑level remedies haven't been attempted, unless there's urgent harm or power imbalance making lower levels impossible.

### What if the other party won't engage?
Document your attempts. The Council may proceed with review based on available information.

### Can I be represented by someone?
You may bring a supporter, but they cannot speak for you in formal proceedings. The process is designed for direct participation.

### How are decisions enforced?
Council decisions are implemented by platform administrators (e.g., suspensions, content removal). The Council's role is to decide; technical enforcement is handled separately.

### What if I'm not satisfied with the outcome?
Appeal to the Guild Council within 14 days (Level 6).

### Can I report something anonymously?
Yes, using the report form. However, anonymous reports may be harder to investigate. If you want to participate in the process, you'll need to identify yourself.

### What about disputes with platform staff?
These go through the same process. Staff are subject to the same Code of Conduct. Appeals of staff actions go to the Moderation Council.

### How are conflicts of interest handled?
Council members with conflicts recuse themselves. The case is assigned to another member. If the entire Council has conflict (rare), the Guild Council may appoint an ad hoc panel.
