# Creating Proposals

Proposals are how your guild translates collective vision into formal requests for government action. They transform the insights from CivicNotes, the templates from the Regenerative Action Library, and the energy of your members into structured, actionable documents that can be submitted to city councils, school boards, and other public bodies. This guide walks you through the entire proposal lifecycle—from initial idea to official submission and beyond.

---

## What Is a Proposal?

A **proposal** is a formal document created by a guild that requests a specific action from a government body. Unlike a CivicNote (which is a brief comment) or a petition (which gathers signatures), a proposal is a comprehensive, well‑reasoned argument for change.

Proposals can be:

- **Standalone** – Created entirely by your guild from scratch.
- **Action‑based** – Adapted from the Regenerative Action Library.
- **Note‑inspired** – Developed from a CivicNote that gained traction.

### What Proposals Include

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Title** | Clear, concise statement of the request. |
| **Background** | Context – why this matters, what problem it addresses. |
| **Proposed Action** | Specific, measurable action requested (e.g., "Adopt a resolution to fund a traffic study"). |
| **Justification** | Evidence, data, community support, alignment with existing plans. |
| **Implementation Considerations** | Timeline, resources, responsible parties. |
| **Success Indicators** | How to know if the action is working (drawn from Regenerative Actions). |
| **Supporting Documents** | Links to notes, petitions, data, endorsements. |

---

## Why Proposals Matter

Proposals are a key **dual‑power** tool. They:

- **Demonstrate organized capacity** – A well‑crafted proposal shows officials that your guild is serious, informed, and representative.
- **Create a paper trail** – Once submitted, proposals become public records that can be referenced and tracked.
- **Anchor accountability** – Proposals feed directly into the Promise Tracker, making it easy to track official responses.
- **Build coalitions** – Multiple guilds can co‑sponsor proposals, showing broad support.

## Proposal Lifecycle
```markdown
Idea → Draft → Internal Review → Guild Vote → (if approved) Submission → Tracking → Outcome
```

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Idea** | A member suggests a proposal topic (from a note, action, or discussion). |
| **Draft** | One or more members write the full proposal text. |
| **Internal Review** | Guild members discuss, suggest edits, and refine. |
| **Guild Vote** | Members vote on whether to adopt the proposal as an official guild submission. |
| **Submission** | If approved, the proposal is submitted to the target government body. |
| **Tracking** | The proposal appears in the Promise Tracker; the guild monitors responses. |
| **Outcome** | The proposal is acknowledged, addressed, or otherwise resolved. |

## Step 1: Generating Proposal Ideas

Proposals can emerge from many sources:

| Source | How It Works |
|--------|--------------|
| **CivicNote** | A note with the "Proposal" intent tag that gains traction. Click "Create Proposal from This Note" on the note's action menu. |
| **Regenerative Action** | Browse the Action Library, find an action that fits your focus, and click "Adapt for My Community." Choose "Create Proposal" as the output. |
| **Guild Discussion** | A member starts a discussion thread: "Should we propose something about [issue]?" |
| **Petition** | If a petition gains significant support, it may be ripe for a formal proposal. |
| **External Input** | A community member or another guild approaches yours to co‑sponsor. |

Once an idea is identified, the next step is drafting.

---

## Step 2: Drafting the Proposal

### Starting Points

**From a Regenerative Action**

If you're using an action from the library, much of the content is pre‑filled:

- **Title** – Based on the action title.
- **Liberate Action** – Becomes the core of your proposed action.
- **Success Indicators** – Ready‑to‑use metrics.
- **Oppress Action** – Provides context on what you're pushing against.

You'll need to **localize** it:

- Add specific location details (e.g., "at the intersection of 5th and Main").
- Reference local data or recent events.
- Identify the exact government body and, if known, the relevant agenda item.

**From Scratch**

If starting fresh, use the **Proposal Editor** in your guild dashboard. It provides a template:
```markdown
[Proposal Title]
Submitted by: [Guild Name]
Date: [Auto‑filled]
Target: [Government Body, e.g., City Council]
Background
[2–3 paragraphs explaining the issue, including relevant CivicNotes, petitions, or community input.]

Proposed Action
[Clear, specific request. Use bullet points if multiple actions.]

Justification
[Evidence: data, testimonials, alignment with city plans, etc.]

Implementation Considerations
[Timeline, resources needed, responsible parties – if known.]

Success Indicators
[How will we know this worked? Use measurable outcomes.]

Supporting Documents
[Links to notes, petitions, endorsing guilds, etc.]
```

### Collaborative Editing

- Multiple guild members can edit simultaneously (like Google Docs).
- Use comments to suggest changes or ask questions.
- Version history is preserved.

### Tips for Strong Drafts

- **Be specific** – Vague proposals are easy to ignore.
- **Use data** – Numbers and evidence build credibility.
- **Connect to existing plans** – If your request aligns with a stated city goal, mention it.
- **Anticipate objections** – Address likely counterarguments.
- **Keep it readable** – Avoid jargon; write for busy officials and community members.

## Step 3: Internal Review

Once a draft is ready, it moves to **Internal Review** in your guild dashboard.
```markdown
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
| PROPOSAL: Traffic Calming Fund for 5th & Main |

Status: Internal Review
DRAFT
[Full proposal text appears here]
COMMENTS (5)
• Alex: "Should we add cost estimates? I can find similar
projects in other cities."
• Jamie: "Good point. I'll add a section on potential funding
sources."
• Priya: "The success indicators look solid."
[Add Comment] [Suggest Edit]
PROGRESS: 4 of 12 members have reviewed
[Move to Vote] [Request More Edits] [Archive]
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

During this stage:
- All members can view and comment.
- Designated editors can make direct changes.
- The review period can be set by an admin (e.g., 7 days).
- Members can indicate they've reviewed (helpful for tracking).

The goal is to refine the proposal until it's ready for a formal guild vote.

---

## Step 4: Guild Vote

When the draft is finalized, an admin or the proposal author can move it to **Vote**.

### Voting Settings

Your guild's voting rules are set in **Settings**. Options include:

| Model | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Simple majority** | More than 50% of votes cast approves. |
| **Supermajority (e.g., 60%)** | Higher threshold for controversial proposals. |
| **Consensus** | No formal vote; discussion continues until no objections. |
| **Representative vote** | Only endorsement managers or elected leaders vote. |

### Voting Period

- Typical duration: 3–7 days.
- Members are notified.
- The proposal page shows live results:
```markdown
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
| VOTE ON PROPOSAL: Traffic Calming Fund |
| |
| Current results: |
| ✅ Yes: 12 · ❌ No: 2 · ⭕ Abstain: 1 |
| Threshold needed: 60% · Current yes percentage: 86% |
| |
| Time remaining: 2 days |
| |
| [Cast Your Vote] |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

### If Approved

The proposal status changes to **Approved** and becomes eligible for submission. The author or admins receive a notification.

### If Not Approved

- The proposal returns to **Draft** or **Internal Review**.
- Feedback from "No" votes is visible (if voters left comments), helping guide revisions.
- The author can revise and resubmit for another vote.

---

## Step 5: Submitting the Proposal

Once approved, you have several submission options:

### Option A: Submit to an Upcoming Agenda Item

If the proposal addresses a specific upcoming agenda item:

1. Go to that agenda item's page.
2. Look for **"Attach Guild Proposal"** (visible to guild admins).
3. Select your proposal from the list.
4. The proposal now appears linked to that agenda item, alongside CivicNotes and petitions.

### Option B: Submit Directly to a Government Body

If there's no matching agenda item (or you want to request a new item):

1. From the proposal page, click **"Submit to [Body Name]"**.
2. Choose submission method:
   - **Email** – Generate a formatted email with the proposal attached; send to the body's clerk.
   - **Print‑ready PDF** – Download a PDF suitable for physical submission.
   - **API submission** – If the body's system integrates with CivicNotes (future feature).

### Option C: Request Agenda Placement

Some bodies allow citizens to request items be placed on future agendas. The proposal can be used as the supporting document for such a request.

### After Submission

The proposal's status updates to **Submitted**. It now appears in:

- The **Promise Tracker** (as a source for future promises).
- Your guild's **Proposals** list (with submission date and any tracking links).
- The relevant agenda item (if attached).

### What to Watch For

- **Official acknowledgment** – When a council member mentions the proposal, a member can propose a promise linking to that statement.
- **Related notes** – Other community members may leave CivicNotes referencing your proposal.
- **Petitions** – Supporters may launch petitions to build pressure.

Your guild dashboard will notify you of any activity related to your proposals.

## Co‑Sponsorship with Other Guilds

Proposals can be stronger when multiple guilds stand behind them.

### How to Co‑Sponsor

1. During drafting, invite other guilds to collaborate:
   - From the proposal editor, click **"Invite Guild to Co‑Sponsor."**
   - Select guilds from the list.
   - They receive a notification and can accept.
2. Co‑sponsoring guilds can:
   - Assign members to collaborate on the draft.
   - Vote on whether to co‑sponsor (using their internal process).
   - Once approved, their guild badge appears on the proposal.

### Benefits of Co‑Sponsorship

- Demonstrates broader community support.
- Shares the workload of drafting and promotion.
- Increases pressure on officials.

## Proposal Statuses

| Status | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **Draft** | Being written; not yet public. |
| **Internal Review** | Open for guild member comments and edits. |
| **Voting** | Guild members are deciding whether to adopt. |
| **Approved** | Passed guild vote; ready for submission. |
| **Submitted** | Formally sent to target body. |
| **Acknowledged** | Official has publicly responded. |
| **Addressed** | Action has been taken (verified). |
| **Withdrawn** | Guild decided not to pursue further. |
| **Expired** | No action taken within reasonable timeframe (e.g., 1 year). |

## Best Practices

### Before You Draft

- **Check existing proposals** – Has another guild already proposed something similar? Consider co‑sponsoring instead of duplicating.
- **Talk to officials informally** – A quick conversation can reveal whether your proposal has a chance and how to refine it.
- **Gather evidence early** – Data, photos, testimonials strengthen your case.

### While Drafting

- **Involve diverse voices** – Ensure your drafting team reflects the community affected.
- **Keep it focused** – One clear ask is better than a laundry list.
- **Use plain language** – Avoid acronyms and insider jargon.
- **Cite sources** – Link to data, news articles, and relevant plans.

### During Review

- **Encourage thorough feedback** – Every member should feel their voice matters.
- **Be open to change** – The best proposals are shaped by many hands.
- **Test readability** – Ask someone unfamiliar with the issue to read it and summarize.

### After Submission

- **Promote your proposal** – Share on social media, in guild discussions, and at public meetings.
- **Recruit supporters** – Ask members to leave supportive CivicNotes on the relevant agenda item.
- **Watch for promises** – Train members to spot official acknowledgment and propose promises.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can individuals submit proposals?
Yes, but guild‑sponsored proposals carry more weight and have access to collaborative tools. Individuals can also use the Regenerative Action Library to create proposals independently.

### How long should a proposal be?
Typically 1–3 pages. Longer if needed, but officials are busy – make every word count.

### What if the government body doesn't have a matching agenda item?
Submit it anyway, or request that it be placed on a future agenda. Many bodies have processes for public input outside of scheduled items.

### Can we submit the same proposal to multiple bodies?
Sometimes – e.g., a proposal about school safety might go to both the School Board and the City Council. Be clear about which body is responsible for which part.

### How do we know if our proposal made a difference?
Track it via the Promise Tracker. If it leads to acknowledgment or action, those will appear. If not, the public record of your proposal remains as evidence of community priorities.

### What if our proposal is rejected?
Rejection is not failure – it's information. You can revise and resubmit, escalate to a higher body, or use it as the basis for a campaign. Every proposal builds the muscle of collective action.
