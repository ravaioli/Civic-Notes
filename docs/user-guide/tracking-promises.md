# Tracking Promises

The Promise Tracker is CivicNotes' accountability engine. It creates a transparent, community‑verified record of when officials acknowledge or act on citizen input. This guide walks you through the entire lifecycle of a promise—from proposing it when you hear an official response, to celebrating when that promise is fulfilled.

---

## What Is a Promise?

A **promise** is a public record that links an official's statement or action back to a specific CivicNote, proposal, or petition. It answers the question: "Did anyone in power actually hear us, and did they do anything about it?"

Every promise includes:
- The original citizen input (CivicNote, proposal, or petition)
- Evidence of official acknowledgment (video timestamp, minutes excerpt, etc.)
- The target official or body
- Current status (Proposed, Verified, Acknowledged, Addressed, or Disputed)
- Community verification votes

---

## Why the Promise Tracker Matters

Before CivicNotes, when an official mentioned a citizen's comment, it usually disappeared into the ether. The Promise Tracker makes these moments visible and permanent.

| Without Promise Tracker | With Promise Tracker |
|------------------------|---------------------|
| Official says "we'll look into it" – then nothing | That statement becomes a **Proposed** promise |
| No one remembers who promised what | All promises are public and searchable |
| Officials face no consequence for inaction | Community verification creates accountability |
| Hard to show impact of civic engagement | Your profile shows promises you've influenced |

## Promise Lifecycle Overview

A promise moves through five stages:
```markdown
Proposed → Verified → Acknowledged → Addressed
↓
Disputed
```

| Status | Meaning | Who Changes It |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Proposed** | Someone has submitted evidence of an official acknowledgment, but it hasn't been verified yet. | Any user |
| **Verified** | Enough community members have reviewed and confirmed the evidence. | Community vote |
| **Acknowledged** | The official has publicly responded (optional step). | Official or community (with evidence) |
| **Addressed** | Action has been taken – the promise is fulfilled. | Community (with evidence) |
| **Disputed** | Community members question the accuracy; under moderator review. | Moderators |

---

## Step 1: Proposing a Promise

You can propose a promise whenever you witness an official acknowledging a citizen's input.

### When to Propose

Good moments to propose a promise:
- A council member says "I agree with the comment about..." during a meeting
- An official cites a specific CivicNote in their remarks
- A staff report references community feedback
- A resolution includes language from a petition or proposal
- Any public acknowledgment that connects official action to community input

### How to Propose

1. Find the **original CivicNote, proposal, or petition** that was acknowledged.
2. Look for the **"Propose Acknowledgment"** button (on the item itself or in the Promise Tracker tab).
3. In the form that opens, provide evidence:
   - **Video timestamp** – Paste a link to the meeting recording with the time (e.g., `https://youtu.be/abc123?t=1245`)
   - **Minutes excerpt** – Copy the relevant text from official minutes
   - **Document upload** – PDF, screenshot, or photo
   - **Quote** – Type the exact words the official said
4. Confirm the **target official or body** (the system may auto‑detect this).
5. Click **"Submit for Verification"**.

The promise now appears with **Proposed** status. You'll receive a notification when the verification period begins.

---

## Step 2: Verifying Promises

Once a promise is proposed, it enters a **verification period** (usually 7 days). During this time, other community members review the evidence and vote on whether it's accurate.

### Finding Promises to Verify

- Your dashboard will show a **"Needs Verification"** section
- Notifications: "A new promise needs your review"
- Guild members may see promises related to their focus areas
- You can browse all active verifications from the Promise Tracker page

### How to Verify

1. Open the promise detail page.
2. Review the evidence (video, document, quote).
3. Ask yourself: **"Does this evidence accurately show an official acknowledging the original community input?"**
4. Click one of the voting options:
   - **Yes, this is accurate** – You believe the evidence supports the promise
   - **No, this is misleading/false** – You believe the evidence is incorrect or exaggerated
   - **Need more information** – You're unsure; leave a comment explaining what's missing
```markdown
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
| VERIFY THIS PROMISE |
| |
| From: Safe Streets at 5th & Main (CivicNote by Alex) |
| |
| Evidence: Video timestamp (Apr 15 City Council meeting) |
| "Council Member Martinez: 'We need to look seriously at |
| traffic calming for 5th and Main. I'll ask staff to include it |
| in the next transportation report.'" |
| |
| [View Full Video] [View Minutes Excerpt] |
| |
| Does this evidence accurately reflect an official acknowledgment?|
| |
| ○ Yes, this is accurate |
| ○ No, this is misleading/false |
| ○ Need more information (comment) |
| |
| [Submit Vote] |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

### Verification Threshold

A promise becomes **Verified** when it reaches the verification threshold, typically:
- **5 "Yes" votes** from community members, OR
- **3 "Yes" votes** that include at least one Guild endorsement

If the threshold isn't met within 7 days, the promise expires and returns to Proposed status (can be resubmitted with stronger evidence).

If enough "No" votes are cast (e.g., 3), the promise becomes **Disputed** and goes to moderators for review.

---

## Step 3: Official Response (Acknowledgment)

Once a promise is Verified, it appears in the target official's dashboard. They have several options:

- **Acknowledge** – The official can click "Acknowledge" to publicly confirm the promise. This updates status to **Acknowledged** and adds their name to the record.
- **Add Comment** – Officials can add a public note (e.g., "Staff is reviewing this; expect an update in 60 days").
- **Mark as Addressed** – If action has already been taken, they can mark it Addressed directly (requires evidence).
- **Ignore** – The promise remains Verified but without official response (this affects their responsiveness score).

### If Officials Don't Respond

The Promise Tracker doesn't depend on officials engaging. If they don't respond, the Verified status remains as a public record of community consensus. Eventually, if action occurs (through other means), anyone can propose an "Addressed" update with evidence.

---

## Step 4: Marking a Promise as Addressed

When the promised action actually happens—a study is funded, a policy changes, a crosswalk is painted—anyone can propose marking the promise **Addressed**.

### How to Mark Addressed

1. Go to the promise detail page.
2. Click **"Propose Addressed"** (visible to all users).
3. Provide evidence of the action:
   - Link to new legislation or resolution
   - Photo of completed work
   - News article
   - Official announcement
4. Submit for verification.

The "Addressed" proposal enters a shorter verification period (e.g., 3 days). Once verified, the promise status updates to **Addressed** (green). This triggers:
- A Φ‑Score boost for the relevant agenda item and district
- Civic Seeds awarded to everyone who contributed (original note author, verifiers, etc.)
- A notification to all involved users
- The promise appears in "Success Stories" feeds

---

## Step 5: Disputes and Moderation

Sometimes promises are contested. If multiple users vote "No" during verification, or if someone flags an existing promise as inaccurate, it becomes **Disputed**.

### What Happens in a Dispute

1. The promise status changes to **Disputed** (red).
2. Moderators (community‑elected users) review the evidence and all comments.
3. Moderators can:
   - **Uphold** – Confirm the promise is valid; status returns to Verified
   - **Overrule** – Declare the promise invalid; status becomes Rejected
   - **Request more information** – Ask for additional evidence
4. The decision is logged publicly with reasoning.

Disputes ensure the Promise Tracker remains trustworthy. Anyone can appeal a moderator decision to the Guild Council.

This means that a fully fulfilled promise significantly boosts the civic health score for the relevant agenda item, district, and even the official's profile.

## Tips for Effective Promise Tracking

### For Proposing Promises
- **Be precise** – Exact quotes and timestamps are strongest.
- **Link clearly** – Make sure the connection between the official's words and the original input is obvious.
- **Don't propose everything** – Save promises for meaningful acknowledgments, not casual mentions.

### For Verifying Promises
- **Watch the evidence** – Take a moment to verify the video or document.
- **Vote thoughtfully** – Your verification helps build community trust.
- **Comment if unsure** – "Need more information" votes trigger helpful discussion.

### For Following Up
- **Check back** – Promises can take months to fulfill; set reminders.
- **Celebrate successes** – When a promise is Addressed, share it!
- **Use promises in advocacy** – A history of unfulfilled promises is powerful evidence for campaigns.

## Promise Tracker Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Proposed** | Initial stage; awaiting community verification. |
| **Verified** | Community‑confirmed as accurate. |
| **Acknowledged** | Official has publicly responded. |
| **Addressed** | Action has been taken (verified). |
| **Disputed** | Under moderator review due to accuracy concerns. |
| **Rejected** | Deemed invalid after dispute. |
| **Evidence** | Video, document, or quote supporting the promise. |
| **Verification Threshold** | Number of votes needed to move from Proposed to Verified. |
| **Civic Seeds** | Reputation points earned by participating in promises. |
