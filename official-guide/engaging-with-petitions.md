# Engaging with Petitions

Petitions represent organized community will. When enough verified residents sign a petition, it becomes a formal request that demands attention. For officials, petitions are both a signal of constituent priorities and an opportunity to demonstrate responsive governance. This guide explains how to find, review, and respond to petitions targeting your body or office.

---

## Why Petitions Matter

| Aspect | Significance |
|--------|--------------|
| **Organized voice** | Petitions represent collective, not individual, concerns. |
| **Verified support** | Signatures are verified (SMS, email, in‑person), ensuring authenticity. |
| **Clear ask** | Petitions state exactly what action is requested. |
| **Public record** | Petitions and responses are visible to the entire community. |
| **Φ‑score impact** | Your response (or lack thereof) affects your responsiveness score. |

Engaging thoughtfully with petitions builds trust and demonstrates that you take organized community input seriously.

---

## Petition Lifecycle from an Official's Perspective
```markdown
Petition Active → Goal Reached → Submitted → Official Response → (if action taken) Addressed
↓
Acknowledged
```

| Stage | What Happens |
|-------|--------------|
| **Active** | Petition gathering signatures; visible in feeds but not yet formally submitted. |
| **Goal Reached** | Signature threshold met; petition prepares for submission. |
| **Submitted** | Petition formally delivered to your body; appears in your dashboard. |
| **Official Response** | You acknowledge, comment, or mark as addressed. |
| **Addressed** | Action taken and verified. |

## Step 3: Responding to a Petition

Once a petition is **Submitted** (goal reached), you have several response options.

### Option A: Acknowledge the Petition

Acknowledgment is a public confirmation that you've received and noted the petition.

**How to acknowledge:**

1. From the petition detail page, click **"Acknowledge."**
2. A response form appears:
```markdown
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
| ACKNOWLEDGE PETITION |
| |
| Petition: Library Hours Extension |
| |
| Your response (optional but recommended): |
| [Thank you to everyone who signed this petition. I've received |
| it and am adding it to the agenda for our June 15 meeting. |
| I encourage everyone to attend or submit public comments. ]
| |
| ☑ Also post this response to the petition page |
| |
| [Cancel] [Confirm Acknowledgment] |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

3. Add your response (or leave blank for a simple acknowledgment).
4. Submit.

The petition status updates to **Acknowledged**, and your response appears publicly.

### Option B: Add a Public Comment

Even before acknowledging formally, you can add comments to engage with petitioners.

**Good uses of comments:**
- Asking clarifying questions
- Providing information about related efforts
- Thanking organizers for their work
- Explaining process or timeline

Comments appear on the petition page and are visible to all.

### Option C: Mark as Addressed (if action already taken)

If the requested action has already been taken (or is in progress), you can mark the petition addressed.

**How to mark addressed:**

1. From the petition detail page, click **"Mark Addressed."**
2. Provide evidence:
   - Link to resolution, ordinance, or budget item
   - Upload document or photo
   - Description of action taken
3. Submit for verification.

The petition enters a short verification period (3 days) for community confirmation. Once verified, status becomes **Addressed** (green).

### Option D: No Response (Not Recommended)

You can choose not to respond, but:
- The petition remains in "Submitted" status indefinitely.
- Your responsiveness Φ‑score will be negatively affected.
- Petitioners may feel ignored, eroding trust.

Even a brief acknowledgment is better than silence.

## Step 4: Responding to Different Types of Petitions

### Petition Requests Within Your Authority

| Scenario | Recommended Response |
|----------|---------------------|
| You support and can act | Acknowledge, state support, outline next steps/timeline. |
| You support but need approval | Acknowledge, explain process (e.g., "This will go to the full Council on June 15"). |
| You need more information | Acknowledge, ask clarifying questions, commit to investigating. |
| You oppose or cannot support | Acknowledge respectfully, explain reasoning, offer alternatives if possible. |

### Petition Requests Outside Your Authority

| Scenario | Recommended Response |
|----------|---------------------|
| Different body (e.g., County) | Acknowledge, explain jurisdiction, forward to appropriate body (and note that you've done so). |
| State/federal jurisdiction | Acknowledge, explain, offer to connect petitioners with relevant representatives. |
| Private entity (e.g., utility) | Acknowledge, explain limits, suggest direct engagement strategies. |

### Sample Response Templates

**Supportive, within authority:**
> Thank you to the [X] residents who signed this petition. I support this request and will be bringing it forward at our [date] meeting. I encourage everyone to attend or submit public comments. You can find the agenda here: [link].

**Needs more information:**
> I appreciate you raising this. Before I can respond fully, could you help clarify whether you're requesting [Option A] or [Option B]? Staff has asked for more specifics to ensure we're addressing the right concern.

**Outside jurisdiction:**
> Thank you for this petition. While this issue falls under [County/State] jurisdiction, I've forwarded it to [Official's Name] and encourage you to also reach out to their office at [contact info]. I'm happy to help facilitate that connection.

**Cannot support:**
> I want to thank everyone who signed this petition for your engagement. After careful consideration, I cannot support this request because [brief, respectful explanation]. However, I'd be open to discussing alternative approaches that might address the underlying concern. Please feel free to reach out to my office.

## Best Practices for Petition Engagement

### Do's

- **Respond promptly** – Within 2 weeks of submission is ideal.
- **Be specific** – "This will be on the June 15 agenda" is better than "We'll look at it."
- **Show appreciation** – Thank people for organizing and signing.
- **Follow through** – If you promised an update, deliver it.
- **Use plain language** – Avoid jargon; write for residents, not lawyers.

### Don'ts

- **Don't ignore petitions** – Silence damages trust and your responsiveness score.
- **Don't make promises you can't keep** – Better to say "I'll investigate" than to guarantee action.
- **Don't get defensive** – Even if you disagree, respond respectfully.
- **Don't use petitions for campaigning** – Keep responses focused on the issue.

## Petitions and the Promise Tracker

Petitions and promises are closely linked:

| Connection | How It Works |
|------------|--------------|
| **Petition creates promise** | When a petition reaches its goal, it automatically creates a Promise Tracker record targeting your body. |
| **Promise references petition** | The promise record links back to the petition as source. |
| **Response affects both** | Your acknowledgment of the petition also affects the related promise. |
| **Addressed status syncs** | When a petition is marked addressed, any related promises also update. |

This integration means that responding to petitions is also responding to promises – efficiency for you, transparency for constituents.

## Common Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Multiple Petitions on the Same Topic

If several petitions address similar issues:
- Acknowledge each individually (different organizers may have worked on each).
- Consider bundling them in your response: "I've received several petitions about library hours. I'm taking them together to the June 15 meeting."
- Track them separately in your dashboard but address the underlying issue comprehensively.

### Scenario 2: Petition Reaches Goal While You're on Vacation

- Your delegates (if appointed) can respond on your behalf.
- If no delegates, respond as soon as you return – add a note acknowledging the delay.
- The system tracks response time; a brief delay is better than no response.

### Scenario 3: Petition Requests Something Already in Progress

Great news! Acknowledge and celebrate:
> "I'm pleased to share that this work is already underway. The Transportation Committee approved a traffic study for 5th and Main at its May meeting. Thank you to everyone who signed – your voices help reinforce the importance of this work."

### Scenario 4: Petition Requests Something Impossible (Legally or Practically)

Be honest but constructive:
> "I appreciate you raising this. Unfortunately, state law prevents cities from [specific action]. However, I'm committed to addressing the underlying concern about [related issue]. I'd welcome a conversation about alternative approaches."

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I have to respond to every petition?
You're not legally required to, but your responsiveness score and public trust will suffer if you ignore petitions that reach their goal.

### What if a petition never reaches its goal?
Petitions that expire without reaching goal do not require official response. However, you may still choose to note the issue if it seems important.

### Can I respond to a petition before it reaches its goal?
Yes. You can add comments at any time. Early engagement can build goodwill and help you understand issues as they develop.

### How are response times calculated?
From the date a petition reaches its goal (becomes "Submitted") to your first acknowledgment or response.

### Can my staff respond for me?
Yes, if you've delegated response permissions. Their actions appear under your name.

### What if I disagree with a petition's premise?
Acknowledge respectfully and explain your perspective. You might say: "I understand the concern, though I see this issue differently because [reason]. I'd welcome a conversation to better understand your perspective."

### How do I know if a petition is verified?
All signatures on CivicNotes petitions are verified (SMS, email, or in‑person). The petition page shows verification breakdown.

### Can I see who signed?
Yes, signers who chose "public" are listed. Some may choose to remain anonymous (counted but not named).
