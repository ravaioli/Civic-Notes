# Guild Governance

A guild is more than a group of people with shared interests—it's a collective with the power to shape civic discourse, influence decisions, and participate in platform‑wide governance. How you govern yourselves internally determines not only your effectiveness but also your legitimacy in the eyes of the community. This guide explores governance models, decision‑making processes, conflict resolution, and the responsibilities that come with being a verified guild.

---

## Why Governance Matters

Good governance ensures that your guild:

- **Makes decisions** that reflect the collective will, not just a few loud voices.
- **Resolves conflicts** constructively before they damage relationships.
- **Remains accountable** to your members and the broader community.
- **Sustains itself** over time, even as members come and go.
- **Builds trust** with other guilds, officials, and the public.

A guild without clear governance risks drift, internal conflict, or capture by a dominant few. A well‑governed guild becomes a trusted, effective force for change.

---

## Governance Principles

These principles, drawn from the Solarpunk Mandala and adapted for CivicNotes, can guide your guild's governance choices:

| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Distributed authority** | Power is shared, not concentrated. Leadership rotates. |
| **Transparency** | Decisions and their rationale are visible to members (and often the public). |
| **Inclusivity** | All members have meaningful opportunities to participate and be heard. |
| **Accountability** | Leaders answer to members; the guild answers to the community. |
| **Adaptability** | Governance evolves as the guild grows and learns. |
| **Subsidiarity** | Decisions are made at the most local level possible. |

## Choosing a Governance Model

There is no single "right" way to govern a guild. The best model depends on your guild's size, culture, and purpose. Below are common models, with their strengths and tradeoffs.

### Model 1: Consensus‑Based

Decisions are made when all members (or all who participate) can support them—not necessarily everyone's first choice, but something everyone can live with.

**How it works:**
- Proposals are discussed until concerns are addressed.
- Members can "block" consensus only if they have a fundamental objection aligned with guild values.
- If consensus cannot be reached, the proposal may be set aside or moved to a fallback voting process.

**Best for:** Small, close‑knit guilds (up to ~15 members) with high trust and shared values.

**Strengths:**
- Builds deep alignment.
- Ensures minority voices are heard.
- Decisions are durable.

**Challenges:**
- Can be slow.
- Requires strong facilitation.
- Can be blocked by a single persistent objector.

### Model 2: Majority Voting

Decisions are made by a vote, with the option receiving more than 50% of votes cast prevailing.

**How it works:**
- Proposals are discussed for a set period.
- A vote is held (simple majority, or supermajority for major decisions).
- Results are binding.

**Best for:** Mid‑sized guilds (15–50 members) where consensus is impractical.

**Strengths:**
- Clear, efficient.
- Scales reasonably well.
- Familiar to most people.

**Challenges:**
- Can create winners/losers dynamics.
- May leave minority feeling unheard.
- Less nuanced than consensus.

### Model 3: Consent‑Based (Sociocracy)

Decisions are made when no member has a "paramount objection"—a reasoned argument that the proposal would harm the guild's ability to fulfill its purpose.

**How it works:**
- Proposals are developed by a small circle or working group.
- Presented to the full guild for consent.
- Objections are treated as gifts—opportunities to improve the proposal.
- If objections are raised, the proposal returns for revision.

**Best for:** Guilds of any size, especially those committed to continuous improvement.

**Strengths:**
- Balances efficiency with inclusion.
- Encourages iteration and learning.
- Reduces adversarial dynamics.

**Challenges:**
- Requires training/practice.
- Distinction between "objection" and "preference" can be tricky.

### Model 4: Representative (Council)

A small elected group makes most decisions, with periodic input from the full membership.

**How it works:**
- Members elect a steering committee or council (e.g., 3–5 people) for a set term.
- The council handles day‑to‑day decisions and proposal development.
- Major decisions (e.g., charter changes, large endorsements) may go to full membership vote.

**Best for:** Large guilds (50+ members) where full participation in every decision is impractical.

**Strengths:**
- Efficient for large groups.
- Clear leadership accountability.
- Members can focus on action, not process.

**Challenges:**
- Risk of disconnection between leaders and members.
- Requires strong election processes.
- Less direct participation.

### Model 5: Hybrid / Adaptive

Many guilds combine elements of multiple models. For example:
- Day‑to‑day decisions by consent among a small coordinating circle.
- Major decisions by full membership vote.
- Charter changes by supermajority.

Your guild can—and should—adapt its governance as you grow and learn.

## Your Guild Charter

Your charter is your guild's constitution. It should document your governance model and key processes. At minimum, a charter should include:

| Section | What to Include |
|---------|-----------------|
| **Mission & Values** | Why your guild exists; what you stand for. |
| **Membership** | Who can join; expectations of members. |
| **Decision‑Making** | How decisions are made (which model, voting thresholds, etc.). |
| **Leadership** | Roles and responsibilities; how leaders are chosen; term limits. |
| **Meeting Rhythm** | How often you meet; how meetings are facilitated. |
| **Conflict Resolution** | Process for addressing internal disagreements. |
| **Amendment Process** | How the charter itself can be changed. |

See [Creating a Guild](creating-guild.md) for a charter template.

## Leadership Roles and Responsibilities

Even in non‑hierarchical guilds, some roles help things run smoothly.

### Common Roles

| Role | Responsibilities | Typical Selection |
|------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Facilitator** | Runs meetings, ensures everyone is heard, keeps discussions on track. | Rotating (e.g., each meeting a different volunteer). |
| **Admins** | Manage membership, guild settings, and platform tools. See [Member Management](member-management.md). | Elected or appointed for a term. |
| **Endorsement Managers** | Oversee the endorsement queue, ensure fair process. | Elected or appointed; may rotate. |
| **Secretary / Scribe** | Takes notes at meetings, maintains records, tracks decisions. | Rotating. |
| **Guild Council Representative** | Represents the guild in platform‑wide governance (Guild Council). | Elected for a set term. |

### Leadership Best Practices

- **Rotate roles** to build shared capacity and prevent burnout.
- **Document processes** so anyone can step into a role.
- **Set term limits** for elected positions (e.g., 1 year, renewable once).
- **Provide support** – New leaders shouldn't be thrown in alone.

## Meeting Rhythms and Formats

Regular gatherings keep your guild connected and moving forward.

### Types of Meetings

| Type | Purpose | Frequency |
|------|---------|-----------|
| **Business meeting** | Make decisions, review proposals, plan actions. | Monthly or bi‑weekly. |
| **Working session** | Collaborate on specific tasks (e.g., drafting a proposal). | As needed. |
| **Social / check‑in** | Build relationships, celebrate wins, discuss concerns informally. | Quarterly. |
| **Annual retreat** | Review year, set vision, update charter. | Yearly. |

### Effective Meeting Practices

- **Publish an agenda** in advance (in your guild's Discussions or Announcements).
- **Assign roles** – facilitator, notetaker, timekeeper.
- **Start with check‑ins** – a quick round of how everyone's doing builds connection.
- **End with clear action items** – who does what by when.
- **Share notes** afterward for those who couldn't attend.

### Sample Agenda
```markdown
Safe Streets Guild · Monthly Business Meeting
April 25, 2025 · 7:00pm ET · [Video Call Link]

Check‑in (5 min)

Approve previous meeting notes (2 min)

Endorsement queue review (15 min)

5th & Main crosswalk note (vote)

School zone speed proposal (discussion)

Proposal update: Traffic Calming Fund (10 min)

Guild Council report (5 min)

Upcoming events / announcements (5 min)

Check‑out / gratitude (3 min)
```

## Decision‑Making Processes

Whatever model you choose, clarity about process prevents confusion and conflict.

### For Routine Decisions (e.g., endorsing a note)

| Step | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Suggestion** | Member flags item in endorsement queue. |
| **Discussion** | Open for comments (e.g., 3 days). |
| **Vote** | Endorsement managers (or all members) vote. |
| **Outcome** | If approved, badge added; if not, item removed. |

### For Major Decisions (e.g., charter changes, large proposals)

| Step | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Proposal** | Draft written and shared at least 7 days in advance. |
| **Discussion** | Dedicated meeting or threaded discussion. |
| **Vote** | Full membership vote (threshold defined in charter). |
| **Implementation** | If passed, changes enacted and announced. |

### Documenting Decisions

Keep a **decision log** (in your guild's Discussions or a shared document) that records:
- Date
- Decision made
- Summary of discussion (key points, not every word)
- Vote outcome (if applicable)
- Action items

This creates institutional memory and transparency.

## Conflict Resolution

Disagreements are natural in any group. How you handle them determines whether they strengthen or weaken your guild.

### Principles for Conflict Resolution

- **Address early** – Small tensions are easier to resolve than entrenched conflicts.
- **Assume good faith** – Most people are trying to help, even when they mess up.
- **Focus on impact, not intent** – "When you did X, I felt Y" is more productive than blame.
- **Seek understanding first** – Before proposing solutions, ensure everyone feels heard.
- **Escalate thoughtfully** – Not every conflict needs formal process.

### A Simple Conflict Resolution Process

| Step | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **1. Direct conversation** | Those involved attempt to resolve one‑on‑one. |
| **2. Facilitated discussion** | If direct conversation fails, a trusted third party (e.g., another member, admin) facilitates. |
| **3. Guild circle** | If still unresolved, bring to a guild meeting for collective input (not decision, but perspective). |
| **4. Mediation** | Seek outside help – another guild's elder, or the Moderation Council. |
| **5. Formal decision** | As last resort, admin team or vote makes a binding decision (e.g., removal). |

### When to Involve the Moderation Council

The Moderation Council handles:
- Code of Conduct violations.
- Disputes that cannot be resolved internally.
- Appeals of guild decisions (e.g., removal).

Before escalating, ensure you've attempted internal resolution and documented efforts.

## Transparency and Accountability

As a verified guild, you have responsibilities to your members and to the wider community.

### To Your Members

- **Open meetings** – All members should know when and how to participate.
- **Accessible records** – Decisions, proposals, and key discussions should be visible (not hidden in private chats).
- **Clear roles** – Everyone should know who is responsible for what.
- **Regular updates** – Admins report on activities and decisions.

### To the Public

- **Your charter** is public.
- **Your endorsed items** are public.
- **Your proposals** are public (once submitted).
- **Your guild's Φ‑contribution** is public.

This transparency builds trust and legitimacy. It also means your governance should be something you're proud to show the world.

### To the Platform (Guild Council)

- Send a representative to Guild Council meetings.
- Participate in platform‑wide decisions.
- Vote in guild verification processes for new guilds.

## Guild Council Participation

Every verified guild is part of the **Guild Council** – a body that helps govern CivicNotes itself.

### Responsibilities

- Electing the Moderation Council.
- Voting on platform‑wide policy changes.
- Allocating collective resources (if any).
- Mediating disputes between guilds.
- Setting strategic direction for the platform.

### How to Participate

1. Designate a **Guild Council Representative** (can be an admin or elected representative).
2. Ensure they attend quarterly Council meetings (or send a proxy).
3. Vote on proposals that come before the Council.
4. Bring issues from your guild to the Council's attention.

See [Guild Council Governance](../governance/guild-council.md) for full details.

## Evolving Your Governance

Your guild's governance should grow with you. Schedule regular check‑ins:

| Frequency | Focus |
|-----------|-------|
| **Quarterly** | Are our processes working? Any pain points? |
| **Annually** | Charter review – does it still reflect who we are? |
| **After major conflict** | What can we learn? Do we need new structures? |

Involve members in these reflections. Governance is not a one‑time design – it's a continuous practice.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can we change our governance model?
Yes. Charter amendments typically require a supermajority vote and advance notice to members.

### What if no one wants to lead?
This is common, especially in newer guilds. Try:
- Rotating small tasks rather than big roles.
- Sharing leadership among a few people.
- Temporarily reducing meeting frequency.
- Asking what support people need to step up.

### How do we handle a toxic member?
Follow your conflict resolution process. If behavior violates Code of Conduct, document and escalate to Moderation Council.

### Can we have no formal leaders?
Yes, many guilds operate with distributed facilitation and no permanent roles. Document how decisions get made so newcomers understand.

### What if our guild is mostly inactive?
Consider:
- Reaching out to members to see if interest remains.
- Holding a "reboot" meeting.
- If truly dormant, consider voluntary dissolution (transfer any resources to Guild Council).

### How do we interact with other guilds?
- Co‑sponsor proposals and petitions.
- Cross‑endorse each other's items.
- Share learnings and best practices.
- Resolve disputes through direct conversation or Guild Council mediation.
